## Topic summary
The Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials (PCAF) maintains the Global GHG Accounting and Reporting Standard for the Financial Industry, the most widely used method for measuring and disclosing financed emissions: the greenhouse gas emissions associated with a financial institution's loans and investments. Financed emissions are calculated by applying an attribution factor (the institution's share of an investee or borrower) to that entity's emissions, then summing across the portfolio. PCAF organizes guidance by asset class and pairs every figure with a data quality score so users can judge how estimated a number is. Recent PCAF guidance extends beyond the core inventory to optional forward-looking metrics, including financed avoided emissions and Expected Emissions Reductions (EER), which is the angle most relevant to Koi.

## Key claims
- The attribution factor allocates an investee or borrower's emissions to the financier in proportion to its stake, calculated as outstanding amount divided by EVIC (Enterprise Value Including Cash) or total equity plus debt -- source: https://carbonaccountingfinancials.com/standard (standard) [differentiated? y]
- PCAF assigns each emissions figure a data quality score from 1 (verified reported data) to 5 (highly estimated, based on sector or building averages); institutions report a weighted data quality score by asset class -- source: https://carbonaccountingfinancials.com/files/downloads/PCAF-Global-GHG-Standard.pdf (standard) [differentiated? y]
- The third edition (December 2025) introduced new methodologies for use-of-proceeds structures, securitizations and structured products, sub-sovereign debt, and optional IFRS reporting of undrawn loans -- source: https://carbonaccountingfinancials.com/files/standard-launch-2025/PCAF-PartA-2025-Full-Document-Clean.pdf (standard) [differentiated? y]
- All PCAF methods build on the GHG Protocol Corporate Standard and the Corporate Value Chain (Scope 3) Standard, and the PCAF Standard is formally recognized by the GHG Protocol -- source: https://ghgprotocol.org/blog/new-standard-developed-help-financial-industry-measure-and-report-emissions (standard) [differentiated? n]
- Financed avoided emissions and EER are optional, forward-looking metrics that sit outside the core financed emissions inventory and are reported separately -- source: https://carbonaccountingfinancials.com/standard (standard) [differentiated? n]
- The asset classes Koi references are seven: listed equity and corporate bonds, business loans and unlisted equity, project finance, commercial real estate, mortgages, motor vehicle loans, and sovereign debt -- source: src/app/docs/data-and-methodology/methodology-alignment/page.mdx (internal) [n/a]

## Company philosophy (SME to fill in)
- when an institutional investor sets "net-zero" targets, it means the portfolio's attributable greenhouse gas emissions—its "financed emissions"—are reduced and balanced to net zero by a target date, typically 2050 or earlier. 
- The core accounting concept is Scope 3 Category 15 (investments): when an institutional investor holds equity or debt in a company, a share of that company's emissions is attributed to the institutional investor based on proportional ownership, usually calculated via PCAF (Partnership for Carbon Accounting Financials) methodologies.
Practically, achieving this involves several intertwined commitments:
Measurement. Inventorying financed emissions across asset classes—listed equities, corporate bonds, private equity, real estate, infrastructure, and increasingly sovereign debt. Each class has its own attribution rules and data challenges; private holdings and sovereigns are particularly thorny.
A decarbonization pathway. Setting interim targets (often 2030) aligned with 1.5°C pathways, frequently anchored to frameworks like the Net Zero Asset Owner Alliance or the Net Zero Investment Framework. This typically means absolute reductions in financed emissions plus increasing capital exposure to climate solutions.
Real-economy vs. paper decarbonization. This is the central credibility question. Portfolio emissions can drop simply by selling high-emitting holdings to other investors, which doesn't reduce actual emissions in the world. Credible commitments therefore emphasize engagement (using shareholder rights to push portfolio companies toward credible transition plans) and capital allocation toward solutions, with divestment used as a signal or last resort rather than the primary lever.
Residual emissions and removals. "Net" zero implies that some residual emissions remain at the target date and are neutralized by high-quality carbon removals—durable sequestration rather than avoided-emissions offsets. This is one of the more contested areas given quality, additionality, and permanence concerns.
Scope. "Net-zero institutional investor" specifically refers to investments. An institution's operational footprint (buildings, travel, etc.) is usually a separate commitment, though some institutions roll both into one institutional target.
A few critiques worth flagging: data quality on portfolio Scope 3 is still patchy; the framework risks rewarding paper decarbonization over real-economy outcomes; net-zero alignment for sovereigns, hedge funds, and derivatives remains methodologically immature; and the line between "transition finance" (legitimately funding hard-to-abate sectors' decarbonization) and continued fossil exposure dressed up in climate language is genuinely hard to police.
The most interesting tension in the space is the gap between portfolio carbon accounting (a balance-sheet exercise) and real-economy impact. The strongest net-zero institutional investor commitments are explicit about how they're closing that gap rather than just optimizing the ledger.
-How would you relate avoided emissions measurements for portfolio climate solutions that avoid emissions from counterfactual scenarios with different baseline technologies (e.g., EVs displacing ICE) ? They still have net positive emissions but are less than if the portfolio included ICE companies. This is exactly where avoided emissions and net-zero accounting create some of the most contested methodological territory in climate finance—and where Project Frame and similar communities of practice have spent real effort. The short answer: avoided emissions are a separate metric that lives alongside, not inside, financed emissions accounting. They cannot be netted against attributed emissions on the path to net zero.
Here's how the two relate:
Different ledgers, different questions. Financed emissions answer "what share of actual, present-day emissions is my capital associated with?" Avoided emissions answer "compared to a counterfactual baseline, how much lower are emissions because this solution exists (or scaled)?" The first is an attributional inventory; the second is a consequential, scenario-based estimate. PCAF explicitly keeps them separate, and the GHG Protocol's draft guidance on avoided emissions (the "Project Frame–adjacent" work, plus WBCSD's guidance) has been firm that avoided emissions don't reduce a company's or portfolio's Scope 1-3 footprint.
Why the separation matters. If an EV manufacturer's tailpipe-displacement avoided emissions could offset the financed Scope 1-3 of the rest of your portfolio, you'd get absurd results: a portfolio loaded with EV makers, heat pump manufacturers, and renewables developers could claim "net negative" while still financing substantial real-economy emissions. The counterfactual is hypothetical; the attributed emissions are (relatively) observed. Mixing them collapses the integrity of both.
Where avoided emissions legitimately fit in net-zero frameworks. They're typically reported as a climate solutions or positive impact metric—often paired with green revenue share, climate solutions AUM percentage, or EU Taxonomy alignment. The Net Zero Investment Framework, GFANZ guidance, and most credible asset owner commitments treat "increasing capital to climate solutions" as a parallel target to "reducing financed emissions," not as a substitute. So an institutional investor might commit to: (1) X% reduction in financed emissions by 2030, AND (2) Y% of AUM in climate solutions, with avoided emissions as the impact metric for the latter.
The practical reconciliation in a net-zero institutional investor. A credible setup looks something like:

Financed emissions tracked and reduced on an absolute basis against a 1.5°C pathway, no avoided-emissions netting.
Climate solutions exposure tracked separately, with avoided emissions as one of several impact metrics (alongside MW deployed, tons of green steel, etc.), reported with full baseline transparency.
An explicit narrative connecting the two: the portfolio is both decarbonizing its attributed footprint and deploying capital to scale solutions whose avoided emissions contribute to system-level decarbonization.

The intellectual honesty point your framing gets at: yes, an EV-heavy portfolio has lower financed emissions today than an ICE-heavy one and contributes to system-level avoided emissions. Both are true and both matter. The discipline is refusing to collapse them into a single number, because the moment you do, the metric stops measuring anything coherent.
